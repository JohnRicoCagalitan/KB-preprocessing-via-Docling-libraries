## III. Academic Year

The  Academic  Year  shall  be  computed  as  the  two  semesters  and  the  following intersession (formerly summer term) of the school year. For those who do not take intersession classes, the academic year shall be computed for the two semesters of the school year.

## IV. Study Load of Undergraduate Students

- A. Regular Student Study Load. The normal study load of regular students is the  number  of  units  prescribed  by  the  curriculum  in  which  the  student  is enrollled  in.  A  student  is  considered  full time or carrying a full load when he carries  all  subjects  prescribed  in  his  curriculum  for  a  particular  school semester. Students should follow the study load prescribed by their curricula or as assigned and approved by their respective Deans.
- B. Probation Study Load. Students on probation are to carry a minimum study load of 15 units and a maximum of 18 units per semester.
- C. Working Student Study Load. Working students are limited to a study load of  15  units  per  semester.  However,  the  Dean  may use his/her discretion to allow a somewhat heavier load which should be less than the full-time/regular load of the student's curriculum.
- D. Graduating Student's Study Load. During the last semester of senior year, a graduating student may be permitted, upon request, to carry a load of three to  six  units  in excess of the regular load, considering the academic standing of the student and upon the recommendation of the College Dean.
- E. Intersession Study Load. A graduate or undergraduate student shall carry a maximum of nine units of work, provided that the student shall carry not more than one laboratory subject, unless otherwise required or prescribed by the curriculum.
- F. Credits for Study Load. To be given credit, all study loads must be explicitly approved by the Dean of the college concerned.

## V. Class Related Regulations

The unit of instruction used for computing the amount of work required for graduation is  the  semester  hour.  One  semester  hour  is  equivalent  to  an  hour's  lecture  or recitation  per  week  for  one  semester.  A  semester  consists  of  18  weeks.  Hours corresponding  to  units  for  laboratory  work  may  vary.  Regular  attendance  in  all classes  and  laboratory  sessions  is  one  of  the  most  important  obligations  of  the student. Students are expected to attend all scheduled exercises of their classes.

For  1st  Bell:  Composed of three successive rings, means the current class should be dismissed

## VI. Examinations, Marks and Quality Point Index (QPI)

- A. Periodic Examinations. Besides regular class quizzes, four oMcial general examinations are given every term: preliminary, mid-term, semi-final and final examinations.  The  dates  of  which  are indicated on the academic calendar. The  schedule  for  each  subject  is  announced  to  the  students  usually  two weeks before the examinations.
- B. Admit  to  Examination  Card. A  student  is  not  allowed  to  take  any  oMcial examination unless s/he presents his/her Admit-to-Exam Card duly stamped as  'cleared'  by  the  Finance  Office.  A  student  using  tampered examination permit  or  one  not  belonging  to  him/her  will  be  punished  severely  with suspension or expulsion.
- C. Examination  Booklets/Answer  Sheets. A  student  must  use  the  oMcial examination  booklet  of  the  University  or  the  prescribed  answer  sheets distributed by the proctor during an examination.
- D. Removal Examination. Absolutely no removal examination will be given to a student.
- E. Final Examination Exemption. Deserving students, whose pre-final cumulative  grade  is  A,  may  be  exempted  from  final  examination.  Upon

exemption, the student will automatically earn a final grade of A. Exemption from final examination is a privilege and not a right of the student.

## F. Delayed Examinations

1. All students who fail to take final examinations shall be given a grade of  Incomplete  (INC).  This incomplete mark should be removed. The deadline  of  compliance  of  INCs  and  the  submission  of  Delayed Grades  across  all  undergraduate  programs  will  be  twenty-five  (25) days after the end of each term. If the INC is not removed within this period, the mark will be automatically changed to F (Failure).
2. Delayed  prelim,  midterm  and  semi-final  examinations are left to the discretion of the teacher. No examination fee shall be collected.
3. The  student  has  to apply for delayed final examination and pay the required examination fee as prescribed by the Finance OMce.
4. The application for delayed final examination shall be approved by the College  Dean  of  the  student.  The Dean shall ascertain the reasons why the student failed to take the final examination. S/he shall indicate on the student's application if the reason is valid or not. Considered valid reasons are: a) tuition fees not paid on time; b) student was sick; c)  urgent  personal  concerns  such  as  death  or  serious  illness  of immediate family. Documentary evidences shall be required. Reasons other than these shall be considered invalid.
5. The administration of delayed final exams should follow the prescribed schedule.  If  this  is  not  possible,  the  student  arranges  the  schedule with  the  faculty.  For  delayed  prelims,  midterms  and  semi-finals,  the student  arranges  the  schedule  with  the  faculty  and  only  for  valid reasons as stated above.
6. If the reason for the delayed final exam is valid, the results of the final examinations  shall  be used in the computation of the student's final grade.
7. If the reason for the delayed final exam is invalid, the student is given sanction  in  the  form  of  point  deduction.  Only  80%  of  the  final examination score shall be used in the computation of the final grade.

## G.  Grading System and QPI Requirements

1. QPI System. The student's promotion, graduation, honors or separation from the College is determined by whether or not s/he has satisfied  the  QPI  requirements  for  a  given  academic  year  which consists of two semesters and the following intersession.
2. The QPI of a student is computed by:
- a) Multiplying  the  quality  point  value  of  the  letter  marks  in  each course by the number of units/credits in the course; and
- b) Adding  the  products  and  then  dividing  the  sum  by  the  total number of units taken during the academic semester or year.
3. NSTP  marks  are  not  included  in  QPI  computations  but  must  be passing marks.
4. Transfer credits from other schools (academic units taken from other schools but credited in XU) are also not included in the computation of QPI.

5. Credits earned from subjects which are not in the student's curriculum but  aligned  with  the  student's  program  of  study  are  included  in  the computation of the QPI.
- H.  Marking System

The mark of the student is computed at the end of the term and is based on quizzes,  long  tests,  oMcial  examinations,  homework  and/or  assignments, periodical tests, recitations, projects and where appropriate, also on practicum. Academic achievement is marked in the following manner:

- -If grade is 'A', the QPI is 4, range in 92-100 percentage, which means 'Excellent'.
- -If  grade  is  'A-',  the  QPI  is  3.5,  range  in 84-91.99 percentage, which means 'Very Good'.
- -If  grade  is  'B',  the  QPI  is  3,  range  in  76-83.99  percentage,  which means 'Good'.
- -If  grade  is  'B-',  the  QPI  is  2.5,  range  in 68-75.99 percentage, which means 'Fair'.
- -If  grade  is  'C',  the  QPI  is  2,  range  in  60-67.99  percentage,  which means 'Satisfactory'.
- -If  grade  is  'D',  the  QPI  is  1,  range  in  50-59.99  percentage,  which means 'Passed'.
- -If  grade  is  'F/AF', the QPI is 0, range in below 50 percentage, which means 'Failed'.

Professional subjects in competency-based programs follow a 60% or C passing grade.

The descriptive equivalents of the letter marks are as follows:

A  Excellent -  The  student  demonstrates  mastery  of  the  subject;  has  exceptional knowledge of the field and complete facility in the application of its principles. A-  Very  Good -  The  student  displays  broad  general  competence in the field; has facility in dealing with both major and minor areas of the subject. B Good -  The  student  demonstrates a good understanding of the principles of the field;  is able to integrate course material with knowledge acquired elsewhere in an average manner. B- Fair - The student has an average grasp of both major and minor principles and shows insight into relationships between topics. C Satisfactory - The student shows understanding of the major topics of the field; is generally competent in the course skills. D  Passed -  The  student  demonstrates  suMcient  knowledge  and  skills  to  assure passing the next  course  in  the  sequence;  shows  minimal  understanding  of  general principles.

F Failed - Failure.

Other marks given and entered in the academic records are: INC Incomplete -  In  extraordinary  circumstances,  when some weighty requirement of a course has not been met or the final examination has not been taken, the student will be given  the  temporary  grade  of  INC  (Incomplete).  All  INCs  should  be complied with within twenty-five (25) days after the end of the term. Otherwise, the grade will automatically be changed to F.

The INC mark is changed to a letter grade through this procedure:

1. Submission of the completed work or of the final examination result
2. Evaluation by the teacher and submission of letter grade to the Department Chair
3. Submission by Department Chair of the delayed grade to the Registrar through channels

W  Withdrawn -  Mark  given  to  the  student  who  withdraws  enrollment  from  the subject/s with the approval of the College Dean, the Registrar and the Finance OMcer within the prescribed period. No quality point.

AF Failure Due to Excess Absences (Overcut) - Mark given to students who incur excessive absences and to those who unoMcially drop or withdraw from the course/s they have enrollled in. Quality point value: 0 (zero).

LOA Leave of Absence - This may be given to a student who files for LOA with the approval of the Dean, the Registrar, and the University Treasurer. No quality point.

## A. Membership in Organization

1.  Currently  enrolled sophomore, junior and senior undergraduate XU students may become members of student organizations. Freshmen, while not encouraged to join may choose to be observers of the organizations of their choice.

In many instances, students are allowed to conduct activities outside the University to enable  them  to  achieve  their goals and serve a valuable purpose. When students wish to conduct activity outside the University, they must accomplish the necessary permits which includes a signed parents/guardian's consent form and submit to the Office of Student Affairs five days before the actual off-campus activity.

## C. Field Trips

Field  trips  in  courses,  if  these  are  parts  of  the  course,  shall  be  scheduled  on non-class days so as not to sacrifice other classes of the students concerned.

1.  If  a  teacher  wishes  to  bring  students  to  field  trips,  he/she  shall  secure  the permission of the Department Chair/Program Director (who judges relevance of the field  trip  to  the  course),  the  Dean  (who  judges  the  merits  of  the  field  trip  vis-a-vis absences in other classes) and the Vice- President for Higher Education (who shall determine  whether  such  trip  does  not  pose  liability  to  the  school).  The  teacher ensures the safety and security of the students.
2.  For  travel  outside  the  city requiring an overnight stay, students shall secure prior written permission of the parents or guardians.
3.  Field trips which take the place of the regular teaching hours shall be reported to the  Department  Chair,  the  Dean  and  Vice-President  for  Higher  Education  in  the Non-Regular Instructional Activities Form.
4.  In  deciding  for  field trips, the relevance of the field trip to the course, the classes the  students  will  miss  during  the  field  trips  and  the  safety  of  the  students  shall  be considered.
5.  Field  trips  undertaken  as  extra-curricular  activities  by  student  organizations  are under the responsibility of the Director of Student Affairs.

## D. Using the Name of the University

Students are allowed to represent the University/use the name of the University or any of its offices and affiliated units including recognized student organizations only when approval is granted by the office concerned. This may apply but may not be limited  to  joining  competitions  or gatherings and organizing activities/events outside the University.

All  student  activities  are  suspended  during  prelim  exams,  one  week  prior  to  and during  midterms,  during  semi-final  exams  and  two weeks prior to and during final examinations.

## VIII. Scholastic Responsibility, Promotion and Separation from the University

## (Application of the QPI System)

A. Scholastic Responsibility. Students enrollled in the University for a degree must achieve  high  academic  performance.  To  be  promoted  or  readmitted  to  the  same program, the student must at least have an average scholastic standing.

## B. Promotion

1.  It  is the student's responsibility to ascertain his/her academic standing at the end of  each  semester/school  term  by  having  himself/herself  evaluated  at  the  Dean's Office concerned.
2. For unconditional promotion to sophomore year, a regular (non-shiftee/non-transferee/non-returnee) freshmen must obtain a year's QPI of 1.50 or above at the end of the academic year.

Freshmen  whose  year's  QPI  falls  below  1.20  will  be  ineligible  for  admission  to Sophomore year.

Freshmen whose year's QPI is 1.20 or more but less than 1.50 may be admitted on probation to Sophomore year at the discretion of the Dean after consultation with the Standards Committee.

Students must write a letter of appeal if they desire this probation.

3.  The  required  QPI  at  the  end  of  Sophomore  year  for  promotion  to  Junior  year is 1.80.
4. The required QPI at the end of Junior year for promotion to Senior year is 2.00.
5. The required year's QPI at the end of the Fourth year for promotion to Fifth year in five-year courses is 2.00.
6. Sophomores whose year's QPI is 1.50 or more but less than 1.80 may be admitted on probation to Junior year at the discretion of the Dean after consultation with the Standards Committee.

Similarly, Juniors (and fourth-year students in five-year courses) may be admitted to the following year if their year's QPI is 1.70 or more but below 2.00 at the discretion of  the  Dean  after  consultation  with  the  Standards Committee. The request must be done in writing.

7. The required year's QPI for Seniors or for Fifth Year students in five-year academic programs for eligibility  for  graduation  is  2.00. Students whose senior year QPI (fifth year QPI for five-year academic programs) is lower than 2.00 may graduate with the XU Diploma if their cumulative QPI for their entire stay in the college at XU is 2.00 or higher.  Students  of  the  new  curriculum  who  do  not obtain the minimum 2.00 QPI requirement for graduation will not be issued a Certificate of Graduation e`ective SY 2021-2022.
8.  A  student  who is  on  probation  is  given  one  year  to  lift  his/her  probation  status. S/he should not take a leave during that year. Otherwise, s/he will be dropped from the college. In cases where a student is able to lift his/her probationary status after one  semester  but  later  fails  to  meet  the  required  QPI  for  his/her  year  level  and semester, s/he may be placed under probationary status again. A student shall not be placed under probation more than two times per academic program.
9.  Students  on  probation  shall  have  minimum  and  maximum  loads  of  15  and  18 academic units respectively for each semester. A student placed on probation shall be  given  the  probation  conditions  in  writing  by  the  College  Dean,  signed  by  the student and a copy sent to the parents/guardians.
10.  The academic evaluation of a student is made on a semestral basis. The QPI evaluation is made on a yearly basis.

## C. Separation from the University

1.  A  student  who  has  been  dropped  from  the  two  academic  programs  shall  be warned  to  improve  his/her  academic  performance  and  move  out  of  probationary status. Otherwise, s/he shall no longer be admitted by any unit in the University.

2.  A  student  who  has  been dropped from three academic programs shall no longer

- be eligible for readmission or admission to any other program in the University.

## XII. Language Policy

A. Bilingualism

In  keeping  our  legitimate  national  aspiration,  XU's  policy  is  bilingualism  which promotes  proficiency  in  both  English  and  Filipino.  While  vocabulary  borrowing  is bound to occur, there should be an attempt to keep each language faithful to its own inner spirit and to prefer the apt to the borrowed word. There must never be mixing of language  structure,  sometimes  called  'code  switching'  which  prevents  mastery of either language and is often an indication of sloppy thinking. In fact, true bilingualism is  mastery  of  both  languages  that  no  need  is  felt  to  keep  switching  from  one language to the other.

Proficiency  in  both  languages  (or  even  in  a  third  language)  does  not  necessarily mean that the two be given equal school time. The main principles determining which language should be used in a particular subject should depend on which language here and now is the most conducive to mastery of the subject matter. Clearly, English subjects should be taught in English and Filipino subjects in Filipino. As for the rest, the necessity and the availability of textbooks and journals (and teachers) will have a part in the decision of which to use in class.

Classes must be taught in one language. Students in a Filipino conducted class are expected to speak entirely in Filipino. English borrowing is accepted when necessary or when dictated by sense.

There  is  no  intention  to  kill  the  native  language  (Inang  Wika)  of  non-Tagalog students. The fact is that many of the students especially here in Mindanao, will turn out to be truly multi-linguals: English, Filipino and the native language (Cebuano-Visayan, Ilongo, etc.). They are also encouraged  to learn foreign languages for which there are facilities provided on campus.

## B. Objectives

The language and literature courses have two distinct objectives to accomplish: first, to impart certain thinking and communication skills and second, to form the minds of the  students  through  contact  with  the  best  literature.  The  two  objectives  may  be conceived as the form and the content of what the old Ratio called Eloquentia.

## C. Skills

The basic skills that have to be acquired are the following:

1. Reading, writing, listening and speaking
2. Clear thinking, analysis and inference
3. Intuition, creativity, synthesis

These skills are themselves the basic tools when studying in college. The skills and habit of reading form the basis of any learning in Science and Philosophy as well as of any further intellectual growth after graduation.

The  skill  much  emphasized  in  Jesuit  education  is  that  of  expression  and  in  the traditional  Ratio,  this  took  the  form  of  public  speaking.  Debates,  plays,  poetry readings, etc. have always been encouraged even as part of curricular activity, the emphasis mainly on the spoken word. The times today may point to other forms of expression,  such  as  electronic  media.  But  the  emphasis  on  expression  and  on training  in  expression  remains  as  urgent.  Expressiveness  is  a  mark  of  the  Jesuit alumnus.

Language proficiency, as the basis of expression, has always been important. Much of  the  proficiency  in  English  of  former  Jesuit  students  came  from  practice  in

translating  the  Latin  classics  into  modern  English  through  acquiring  a  feel  for  the nuances of language and sense of 'style.'

Today, language teachers can take advantage of the bilingualism of the students by having  them  translate  from  one into the other, always insisting that the translation rings  true  culturally,  as  if  it  were  originally  created  in  that  language.  Feel  for  the language will mean greater ability to work within the culture and be effective in it.

## D. Content

Besides training in style, a study of literature for its content can be truly humanizing since  it  places  the  student  in  contact  with  the  greatest  minds  of  the  human  race through the written word.

By entering into the experience of fellow humans, some living in ages very di`erent from our own but caught like us in limitations of culture, one gains insights into one's own situation and can in a true sense be liberated and educated.

Thus, as part of regular class work or simply for pleasure, the student is encouraged to  read  Western  and  Asian  authors  side  by  side  with  Filipino  authors.  Leisure  for reading is the privilege of a relative few yet it almost becomes a duty for those few to bring the culture of other times and other places to their own native land.

## XIII. Institutional Requirements

A.  Orientation  Seminar  (ORSEM). The  orientation  seminar  is  required  for  all  new undergraduate  students  in  the  University. The seminar which runs for three days aims to introduce  new  students  to  the  mission,  vision  and  policies  of  the  University.  Proper orientation  clearance  from  the  Office  of  Student  A`airs  has  to  be  obtained  before  new students can claim their University ID. Delayed orientation is held during the first week of regular  classes  for  those  who  were  not  able  to  attend  the  regular  orientation  due  to legitimate reason. Those required to attend the delayed orientation will be marked absent from the classes they will miss.

The  second  semester  orientation  is  held  during  the  first  week  of  regular  classes  and  is patterned  after  the  delayed  orientation  format.  The  orientation  of  graduate  students  is conducted by their respective Program Directors or Deans.

B. Ignatian Culture and Mission (IDE 10.1) - This is a required Interdisciplinary Elective 1 for  all  programs.  In  this  course,  the  students  are  introduced  to  the  di&lt;erent  themes  of Ignatian  culture  and  its  mission.  They  are  led  to  understand  and  appreciate  heritage, tradition, values, attitudes, spirituality, leadership, involvement and service. The students are invited to take on the challenge to nurture a life of discernment, prayer and service of God, others, society and the world.

C. Physical Education (PE) Program. All students are required to take and pass four units of basic PE during the first two years.

1. Only one basic PE subject may be taken in a semester.
2. No student is exempted from PE program. Physically-handicapped students are assigned activities suited for their conditions.

D. National Service Training Program (NSTP). In conformity with government regulations, XU requires all students to register for two semesters of NSTP during the regular enrollment period. Graduates of the Literacy Training Service (LTS) and Civic Welfare Training Service

(CWTS) are organized into the National Service Reserve Corps (NSRC). NSRC Reservists are  required  to  physically  register  in  their  respective  schools/barangays  of  residence  and secure  a  school/barangay certification that s/he is registered and accounted for. The said certification is a requirement for college/program graduation.

## E. Recollections and Retreat Requirements

1.  XU  is  committed  to  the  faith  development  of  its  students.  This  is  in  accord  with  the educational  philosophy  that  education  is  to  contribute  to  the  full  formation  of  the  human person engaging him/her not only intellectually, but as well as ethically, psychologically and spiritually.
2. For reflection on ethical, moral and religious truths and practice, with the thrust of forming its  students  to  be  holistic,  integrated  persons  of  faith,  XU  requires  certain  number  of recollections  and  retreats  for  all  undergraduate  college  students.  The  recollections  and retreats  are  facilitated  by  the  Campus  Ministries  OMce  (CMO)  in  coordination  with  the Department of Religious Studies (RS) and other formation units in the University.
3. The formation program is applicable to all students under the baccalaureate, graduate and professional programs.
4. Students in every level should go through the experience of the spiritual formation through the recollections and retreats and the Graduate School students should have at least two spiritual formation sessions.
5. All undergraduate students of XU are required to undergo two live-in retreats (Junior and Senior's Retreat).
6.  In  the  conduct  of  the  recollection  and  retreat,  students  who  are  not  Catholics  are  not required to attend the specifically Catholic liturgies in regard to Sacraments of Reconciliation and  the  Eucharist.  However,  while  not  attending  these  Catholic rites, they will have their separate  prayer  sessions  designed  and  implemented  by  CMO  in  consultation  with  the students' appropriate church leaders. In sum, as XU is concerned with the activities for full human formation of its Catholic students, it is concerned as well with the same fullness of value formation for all who choose to take its educational programs without proselytizing. In the  spirit  of  inter-faith  and  economical  dialogue,  the  University  avoids  and  discourages proselytizing by any group.
- F.  Other Requirements. Other  requirements may be announced by the University through the  appropriate  oMces.  College  Deans  and  Department  Chairs  shall  regularly  guide  the students in the fulfilment of their academic requirements.
